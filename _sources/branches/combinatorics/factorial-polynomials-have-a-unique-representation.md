A [factorial polynomial][bookofproofs$8418] `$\phi(x)$` of degree `$n$` has a unique representation  `$$\phi(x)=a_nx^{\underline{n}}+a_{n-1}x^{\underline{n-1}}+\ldots+a_1x^{\underline{1}}+a_0,\quad a_n\neq 0,$$` that is, if `$\phi(x)$` has some other representation `$$\begin{align}\phi(x)&=b_{n+k}x^{\underline{n+k}}+b_{n+k-1}x^{\underline{n+k-1}}+\ldots+b_{n+1}x^{\underline{n+1}}+\nonumber\\
&\quad+b_nx^{\underline{n}}+b_{n-1}x^{\underline{n-1}}+\ldots+b_1x^{\underline{1}}+b_0\nonumber\end{align},$$` then `$$b_{n+k}=b_{n+k-1}=\cdots=b_{n+1}=0$$` and `$$b_r=a_r,\quad r=0,1,\ldots,n.$$`
