Chronology

1701

Anders Celsius

Charles Marie de La Condamine
A French mathematician and surveyor who took part in an expedition to South America to measure a degree of latitude.
William Emerson
William Emerson published textbooks which popularised the work of Isaac Newton.

1702

Thomas Bayes
An English clergyman who set out his theory of probability in 1764. His conclusions were accepted by Laplace in 1781, rediscovered by Condorcet, and remained unchallenged until Boole questioned them. Since then Bayes' techniques have been subject to controversy.

1703

Antoine Deparcieux
A French mathematician who is best known for an early work on annuities and mortality.

Augustin Hallerstein

Jean-Louis Calandrini

1704

A French mathematician who worked on the calculus of variations.
Gabriel Cramer
Gabriel Cramer worked on analysis and determinants. He is best known for his formula for solving simultaneous equations.
Johann Francesco Melchiore Salvemini Castillon
An Italian mathematician and astronomer who wrote on the cardioid.
Johann Andreas von Segner
A Hungarian mathematician who was the first professor of mathematics at Göttingen. He made substantial contributions to the theoery of Dynamics.

1706

Benjamin Franklin

Benjamin Robins
An English engineer who wrote on Newton's mathematics and invented the ballistic pendulum.

Émilie du Châtelet

1707

Georges Louis Leclerc Comte de Buffon
A French scientist who was important in the area of natural history. His needle experiment caused much discussion about probability.
Leonhard Euler
One of the most versatile mathematicians of all time. He made enormous contibutions to a wide range of mathematics and physics and left extensive works on curves, series, variation calculus, calculus, geometry, algebra, and on diverse topics of technology, mechanics, optics, and astronomy.

Vincenzo Riccati

Ruggero Giuseppe Boscovich

An astronomer who produced tables of the location of planets and their satellites. He also worked at the bureau of longitudes.

Pierre-Simon Laplace

Adrien-Marie Legendre's major work on elliptic integrals provided basic analytical tools for mathematical physics. He gave a simple proof that $\pi$ is irrational as well as the first proof that $\pi^2$ is irrational.

1763

Posthumous discovery of Bayes' theorem
In a letter of Richard Price to John Canton1 he communicates his discovery of an unpublished essay of already deceased Bayes, titled "An Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances", which he finds of "great merit, and well deserves to be preserved." The discovered essay contains a theorem named after the death of Bayes the Bayes' theorem.
