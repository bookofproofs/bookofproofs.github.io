Proof

(related to Proposition: Uniqueness of Inverse Rational Numbers With Respect to Multiplication)

  • For a given rational number \(x\in\mathbb R\), \(x\neq 0\), let \(y\) be any rational number with $xy=1\label{E18324}\tag{1}.$
  • Applying the existence of inverse rational numbers with respect to multiplication, we can multiply both sides of the equation by \(x^{-1}\) from the left side, resulting in $x^{-1}(xy)=x^{-1}\cdot 1.$
  • From the existence of rational one and since the multiplication of rational numbers is cancellative, it follows that $x^{-1}(xy)=x^{-1}.$
  • Because the multiplication of rational numbers is associative, it follows $(x^{-1}x)y=x^{-1}.$
  • Applying the existence of inverse rational numbers with respect to multiplication once again leads to $1\cdot y=x^{-1}.$
  • Using the existence of rational one once again, results in $y=x^{-1}.$
  • Thus, the there is only one such rational number $y$ for which $x\cdot y=1$, namely $y=x^{-1}$.
  • Note that because the multiplication of rational numbers is commutative, we would get the same result, if we multiplied both sides of the equation $\eqref{E18324}$ from the right side by \(x^{-1}\).
